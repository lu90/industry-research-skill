# Valuation Logic

Use valuation only after lifecycle judgment.

## Lifecycle-Based Valuation

| Stage | Primary logic | Caution |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | Option value, future market space, business-model validation | Very sensitive to assumptions. |
| Growth | Revenue growth, market share, scalability, long-term margin | High growth can mask weak unit economics. |
| Maturity | Stable profit, cash flow, cycle position, comparable multiples | Growth slowdown can compress multiples. |
| Decline | Cash-flow harvesting, liquidation/asset value, restructuring | Low valuation can be a trap. |

## Common Methods

Use PE for profitable businesses, PS for high-growth profit-immature businesses, PB for asset-heavy financial/cyclical businesses, EV/EBITDA for capital-intensive comparison, DCF for modelable long-term cash flow, sum-of-the-parts for multi-business companies, and asset/replacement value for some cyclical/resource/infrastructure/distressed contexts.

## Funding and Market Conditions

Valuation is affected by fundamentals, risk-free rates, liquidity, risk appetite, sector crowding, narrative, and expectation gaps. Distinguish fundamental improvement from valuation expansion caused by liquidity or sentiment.

## Scenario Rules

Use base/upside/downside scenarios for uncertain industries. State assumptions. Do not present scenarios as predictions. Explain what evidence would falsify the valuation logic.

## Output Rules

State why the selected valuation logic fits the lifecycle, use comparable companies only when business models and stages are comparable, use segment valuation when needed, and warn about low valuation traps in declining industries.
